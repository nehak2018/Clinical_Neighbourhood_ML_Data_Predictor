🚀 Step-by-Step: What To Do Next
✅ Step 1: Explore Your Data (Descriptive Phase)

Before ML, always understand your data.

Do:

Check summary statistics

Look at correlations

Plot diabetes prevalence distribution

Check for unrealistic values

Example questions:

Does poverty correlate with diabetes?

Does age 65+ correlate positively?

Does income correlate negatively?

This validates that your synthetic relationships behave logically.

✅ Step 2: Define Your Prediction Goal

Decide clearly:

Option A — Current Prediction

Predict:

Diabetes prevalence using neighborhood features.

Option B — Forecasting (Stronger)

Predict:

Diabetes prevalence in 2024 using 2022–2023 data.

For forecasting, create:

lag_diabetes_prev = previous year's prevalence

That makes it a time-aware ML model.

✅ Step 3: Split Data Properly

If forecasting:

Train on:

2022–2023

Test on:

2024

Do NOT randomly split by row if doing time prediction.

Time-based split is critical.

✅ Step 4: Train Your ML Model

Start simple:

Random Forest (baseline)

Then XGBoost (recommended)

Model inputs:

median_income

poverty_rate

age_65_plus

unemployment_rate

pm25

food_access_index

lag_diabetes_prev (if forecasting)

Target:

diabetes_prev

✅ Step 5: Evaluate Performance

Use:

RMSE

MAE

R²

Ask:

Is model learning meaningful patterns?

Is prediction error reasonable?

✅ Step 6: Interpret the Model

Very important for publication.

Use:

Feature importance

SHAP values (if using XGBoost)

You want to show:

Poverty and age structure are strongest predictors.

That strengthens your manuscript.

✅ Step 7: Forecast Future Years

If using lag variable:

Use 2024 predictions

Feed them into model to predict 2025

Continue recursively

Now you have:

Predicted future neighborhood diabetes prevalence.

🎯 What Your Hybrid Pipeline Now Looks Like

Synthetic Observed Prevalence
→ Train ML Model
→ Validate
→ Interpret
→ Forecast Future

You are officially in the ML phase.
